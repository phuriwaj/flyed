The quote you receive from an operator is rarely the full cost. Understanding what is included, what is not, and what additional expenses will appear is essential for honest parent communication and successful fundraising.

## What operators typically include

Ground costs — accommodation, ground transport, activity fees, guide services, some meals — are usually the largest visible line item. In Thailand, these costs are competitive and typically range from $900 to $1,800 per student depending on accommodation standard and program complexity.

The breakdown of ground costs for a two-week northern Thailand service-learning trip:

- Accommodation (mid-range hotels, shared rooms): $400–$600 per student
- Ground transport (air-conditioned coach, all transfers): $120–$180 per student
- Activity fees (service project access, guide, materials): $150–$250 per student
- Meals (breakfast and dinner daily, some lunches): $150–$250 per student
- Guide and coordinator fees: $80–$150 per student

## What operators often exclude

Flights — international airfare to Bangkok and onward to Chiang Mai typically adds $700–$1,100 per student depending on departure city and booking timing. This is frequently not in the operator quote.

Travel insurance — comprehensive school trip insurance runs $80–$150 per student for a two-week trip. This must be budgeted separately.

Visa fees — most nationalities do not require a Thai tourist visa for stays under 30 days. Check the specific requirements for your student and staff nationalities.

Tips and gratuities — local customs expect tips for guides, drivers, and homestay families. Budget approximately $30–$50 per student for the trip.

Personal expenses — souvenirs, additional meals, snacks, phone data. Advise students to carry $100–$200 in personal spending money.

## The real all-in cost

For a UK or US school group traveling to northern Thailand:

- Ground program: $1,200–$1,800 per student
- International flights: $700–$1,100 per student
- Travel insurance: $80–$150 per student
- Tips and personal expenses: $130–$250 per student
- **Total: approximately $2,200–$3,400 per student**

Smaller groups (under 15 students) pay a premium — operators add a group-size supplement. Larger groups (over 30) can negotiate discounts.

## Fundraising strategies

Fundraising is the most common way schools close the cost gap for families who cannot pay the full amount. Effective approaches that have worked for schools we work with:

**Direct fundraising** — school-organized events, bake sales, sponsored activities. Our experience suggests $200–$500 per event for a secondary school community.

**Trust and foundation grants** — many education trusts fund international school trips as part of their cultural exchange or global citizenship mandates. Applications typically require 8–12 weeks lead time.

**Parent payment plans** — spreading the cost across three or four installments reduces the immediate financial burden and increases completion rates.

**Bursary funds** — schools with existing bursary budgets should allocate a portion to international trips. The educational case for inclusion is strong.

## What low-cost operators cut

If a quote looks too low, it probably is. Cost reductions typically come from: partner organization payments (NGOs should be paid, not subsidized by free student labor), staff ratios (fewer guides means lower cost but higher risk), accommodation standards (clean but basic), and food quality (buffet catering is cheaper than restaurant meals).
