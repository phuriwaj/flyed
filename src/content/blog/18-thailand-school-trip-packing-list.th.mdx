The packing list sounds simple. Students go to Thailand, they need clothes and toiletries and a few specifics for the activities they are doing. In practice, packing mistakes are consistent across groups: too much clothing, not enough of what actually matters, and items that are useless or dangerous in the Thai environment.

Here is the list built from years of feedback from students and trip leaders.

## Documents and money

Passport (valid for six months beyond the trip end date). Flight tickets (printed and digital copies stored separately). Travel insurance certificate (physical copy and digital). Student ID card. Small amount of Thai baht for arrival — approximately 1,000 THB covers incidentals before ATMs are accessed.

No student should carry more than 5,000 THP in cash at any time. ATMs are widely available in cities and tourist areas.

## Clothing

Thailand is hot and humid year-round. Students pack lightweight, breathable clothing in light colors. Synthetic athletic fabrics dry faster than cotton and are worth the investment.

Essential items: 5–7 t-shirts, 2–3 pairs of lightweight trousers or long shorts (required for temple visits — shorts above the knee are not permitted), 1 light rain jacket ( monsoon season, June–October), 1 long-sleeve shirt for evenings in hill areas like Chiang Mai, 2 pairs of sturdy walking shoes (these get wet — do not bring expensive shoes), 2–3 pairs of socks, underwear for the duration plus 2 spare pairs, swimsuit.

Students should not bring more than two weeks of clothing. Laundry is available in most locations and costs between 30–80 THB per kilogram.

## Toiletries

Sunscreen SPF 50+ (expensive in Thailand, bring from home), insect repellent with DEET (Thai mosquito products are less effective), personal medications (enough for the trip plus a spare week), prescription medications in original packaging with doctor's letter, basic toiletries for the duration.

Electrical items: Thai power sockets are Type A/B (same as US) and Type C/F (same as Europe). UK travelers need a universal adapter. A power bank for phones is useful for long travel days.

## Activity-specific items

Service-learning projects: sturdy closed-toe shoes that can get dirty, old clothes for work days, work gloves if your project involves manual work.

Diving or water activities: a rash guard (UV protection and prevents jellyfish stings), reef-safe sunscreen (required at some dive sites), a small dry bag for protecting valuables during boat transfers.

Homestay: a small gift for the host family is a customary gesture. Something representative of the student's home culture — food items, crafts, photographs. Value does not matter; the gesture does.

## What not to bring

Expensive jewelry or watches. More than two smart devices. Valuables that would be painful to lose. A suitcase full of "just in case" clothing that will not get worn.

## Technology and communication

Smartphones work on Thai networks with a local SIM or international roaming. We recommend a local AIS or True Move SIM for data — available at Bangkok airport on arrival for approximately 300 THB. Students using school-issued devices should check that their devices are unlocked before departure.

International calls: WhatsApp and LINE (popular in Thailand) work over WiFi without a local number.

## The personal medication question

All prescription medications must travel in original pharmacy packaging with the student's name and dosage clearly labeled. A doctor's letter in English explaining the medication and its purpose is required for Thai customs. This is particularly important for medications that are common in the UK or US but restricted in Thailand — some ADHD medications fall into this category.

Students with asthma, severe allergies, or other chronic conditions must carry their emergency medication (inhaler, EpiPen) in their hand luggage, not checked luggage.
