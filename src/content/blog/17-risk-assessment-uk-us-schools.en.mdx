Risk assessment is not paperwork. It is the thinking process that keeps students safe. For Thailand school trips, the risk assessment must address hazards that are context-specific to Southeast Asia — dengue fever, road traffic, water activities, and cultural misunderstanding — alongside standard trip risks.

## UK risk assessment requirements

UK schools operate under the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and associated regulations for educational visits. The DfE guidance document "Health and Safety on Educational Visits" (2018) sets the framework, and most schools also follow the OEAP National Guidance for school trips.

A compliant risk assessment for an overseas trip must identify:

- The activity or location being assessed
- The hazards present
- Who is at risk (students, staff, contractors)
- Current control measures in place
- Any residual risk rating
- The person responsible for monitoring

For Thailand specifically, risk assessments must address: vector-borne disease (dengue, Zika), motorbike traffic, water activities, UV exposure, food safety, heat illness, natural disaster risk (monsoon flooding in some regions), and political stability in the far south near the Malaysian border.

The risk assessment must be approved by the headteacher and typically by the governing body for trips outside Europe. It should be reviewed and updated as the itinerary is finalized — not written once and left unchanged.

## US risk assessment requirements

US schools operate under state-specific regulations, which vary significantly. Most states require board approval for overnight out-of-state or international trips. Some states (New York, California, Texas) have specific regulations on foreign travel programs.

US schools with IB programmes must also ensure that any school-sponsored international travel meets IB's requirements for service and academic activities — particularly if the trip is being documented for CAS or MYP credit.

The American Association of School Administrators guidance recommends that international trip risk assessments address: travel insurance adequacy, medical pre-screening of students, host-country safety and political conditions, emergency communication plans, and crisis response procedures.

## Thailand-specific hazards

**Road traffic** — Thailand has one of the world's highest road fatality rates. The hazard is not negligent driving — it is traffic density, road quality, and driving culture that differs significantly from the UK or US. Students must be briefed on road-crossing procedures, and coach transport should use reputable companies with seatbelt compliance.

**Dengue fever** — Transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes, dengue is endemic in Thailand and has no widely available vaccine for all strains. Prevention relies on insect repellent, long sleeves at dawn and dusk, and accommodation with air-conditioned rooms (mosquitoes cannot fly in cold air). The risk assessment must include a vector-borne disease prevention plan.

**Water activities** — Open-water swimming, kayaking, and diving carry drowning risk. Each water activity requires a specific risk assessment sub-section with controls including: supervision ratios, student swimming ability assessment, equipment checks, and emergency rescue procedures.

**Heat illness** — Thailand is tropical and can be physically punishing for students from temperate climates. Heat illness risk is highest in March through May. Control measures include: hydration protocols, shaded rest periods, activity timing (no outdoor activity 11am–3pm during hot season), and staff training on heat exhaustion symptoms.
