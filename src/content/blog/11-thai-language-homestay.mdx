For MFL (Modern Foreign Language) coordinators, two weeks of Thai immersion sounds appealing in theory and terrifying in practice. Here is what an actual flyed program looks like, day by day.

## The shape of the program

Fifteen hours per week of classroom instruction combined with a homestay in a vetted Chiang Mai family, plus cultural activities and weekend excursions. Class size is capped at 8 students per tutor, with two tutors per group of 16 students. This ratio matters enormously — it is the difference between students getting meaningful correction and students slipping through the program unnoticed.

## Week 1: building the foundation

Days 1 through 3 focus on tones, the Thai alphabet, and survival phrases. Students spend time inside and outside the classroom absorbing the writing system, which is a hurdle that English speakers consistently underestimate. By day 3, most students can read simple signage.

Days 4 and 5 shift to food vocabulary and market transactions. Students buy produce at Warorot Market with their tutor watching but not intervening. This is deliberately challenging — haggling in a second language in front of strangers is uncomfortable, and that discomfort is part of the learning.

Day 6 is the first homestay weekend. Students move into their assigned families. Many describe the first 24 hours as overwhelming. By hour 36, they are usually managing basic meals and greetings without prompting.

## Week 2: moving into production

Days 8 through 10 introduce travel phrases and present-tense storytelling. Tutors start requiring students to narrate their day in Thai, first in structured exercises and then in open conversation.

Day 11 is a half-day field trip where students must navigate Chiang Mai using only Thai — no English, no translation apps. They move through three checkpoints: a restaurant, a local transport booking counter, and a landmark inquiry. Tutors shadow at a distance but do not help unless there is a safety concern.

Day 12 is presentation day. Each student delivers a short spoken presentation in Thai to their peers on a topic of their choice. Day 13 closes with a formal assessment, a celebration event with host families, and a certificate ceremony.

## What students exit with

Students typically emerge at A1 to A2 on the CEFR scale depending on their starting level. They receive a transcript signed by the language school, which is accepted by most UK and international schools for MFL credit. Each student also leaves with daily journals, audio recordings, and a portfolio of written work — useful evidence for university applications and school reports.

## What school trip programs get wrong

Shorter programs under 10 days rarely allow meaningful progress. Students learn survival phrases, feel good about the experience, and retain almost nothing within three months. Programs without vetted homestays leave students in hotel rooms where they speak English to each other between activities — the homestay element disappears entirely. Programs without structured classroom hours use the homestay as a substitute for formal instruction, which does not produce measurable language outcomes.

Our approach pairs structured classroom time with genuine homestay integration. We do not run programs shorter than 12 days, and every homestay family is visited and assessed before a student is placed.

If you are evaluating Thai language programs for your school, ask specifically about daily classroom hours, homestay vetting processes, and the ratio of students to native speakers in informal settings. Those three data points tell you more than any brochure.
