The homestay is not a logistics solution for accommodation. It is a pedagogical model with documented outcomes in language acquisition, intercultural competence, and personal development. When schools treat it as a budget option, they miss the point entirely.

## What the research says

Intercultural competence development is measurable. The Intercultural Development Inventory (IDI) has been used in study-abroad contexts to track growth in intercultural sensitivity. Students who complete structured homestay programs show statistically significant improvement on the IDI compared to classroom-only international experiences.

The mechanism is contact depth. Gordon Allport's contact hypothesis — the foundational framework in intergroup relations research — predicts that sustained, equal-status contact across cultural boundaries reduces prejudice and builds empathy. A homestay creates exactly the conditions Allport described: shared daily life, equal-status interaction, common goals, and institutional support.

## The Thai family context

Thai family culture creates particularly productive conditions for student homestays. Thai hospitality norms mean students are treated as guests, which on one level creates inequality — but the hospitality is genuine, and Thai families are experienced at managing guest expectations.

The practical result is that students experience Thai daily life from the inside rather than as observers. They eat Thai food for every meal. They participate in whatever is happening — a Buddhist ceremony, a rice harvest, a local market visit, a funeral procession. They use Thai language because their hosts' English is limited and the situation requires communication.

## Language learning in the homestay context

Classroom Thai instruction produces recognition vocabulary. Homestay Thai produces communicative competence. The difference matters when students return to the classroom or continue to university language study.

Our homestay programs in Chiang Mai and Yasothon provinces pair students with families who have hosted school groups for at least five years. We brief the families on student language level before arrival so they can calibrate their communication approach. This is not a common practice — most operators simply book families and hope for the best.

## Structuring the homestay for learning

A homestay without structure produces experiences without learning. We use a three-night minimum — anything shorter and students spend the first night adapting rather than engaging.

The structure includes: a pre-departure language module on daily-use phrases and politeness conventions; a daily reflection prompt completed each evening in a personal journal; a host-family interview task that students complete on day two, asking the family about their history and daily routine; and a group debrief on the final morning.

## What students say afterward

Students who complete homestay programs consistently cite specific moments: cooking with their host mother, playing with neighbourhood children, attending a local temple with the family. These moments are not incidental. They are the learning. The classroom prepares students to have them. The homestay creates the conditions for them to happen.
