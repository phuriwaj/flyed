Risk assessment is a legal requirement for school trips in most UK and US jurisdictions. It is also good practice. A properly constructed risk assessment does not eliminate adventure — it ensures that decision-makers understand the risks they are authorising and have put appropriate controls in place. Here is what coordinators need to know when planning a Thailand trip.

## UK requirements

In the UK, the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and associated pupil safety guidance (DfE guidance on health and safety on educational visits) place a duty of care on schools for all activities involving their students, including trips abroad.

Schools must complete a written risk assessment that identifies hazards, assesses risk, specifies control measures, and names responsible persons. For overseas trips, the risk assessment must also cover travel, accommodation, local medical facilities, and communication plans.

The Outdoor Education Advisers' Panel (OEAP) provides national guidance that most UK schools follow. Their guidance specifies that risk assessments should be activity-specific and provider-specific — a generic Thailand risk assessment is insufficient.

**Key elements for a UK-compliant risk assessment:**
- Named trip leader and deputy leader
- Staff-to-student ratios for each activity type
- Medical information for all students, including allergies and ongoing conditions
- Communication protocol between the trip and school contact at home
- Emergency procedures including evacuation plan
- Insurance verification confirming coverage for all planned activities
- Pre-departure briefings for students, staff, and parents

## US requirements

US school trip requirements vary by state and school district, but the general framework is similar. Schools operate under a duty of care standard, and negligence claims arising from inadequate trip planning have resulted in significant liability findings in US courts.

Most US schools require parental permission for out-of-country travel, and many districts require board or administration approval for international trips. The risk assessment process is typically less formally codified than in the UK, but the underlying duty of care is the same.

**Key elements for a US school trip risk assessment:**
- Detailed itinerary with all activities named
- Medical and emergency contact forms for all participants
- Staff supervision plan including overnight supervision arrangements
- Background checks confirmed for all adult supervisors
- Travel insurance with medical evacuation coverage
- Emergency protocol including evacuation and natural disaster procedures
-Communication plan with embassy or consular registration

## Thailand-specific risk considerations

Beyond the standard elements, Thailand trips require specific attention to several areas:

**Dengue fever and mosquito-borne illness:** Transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes, which are active during daylight hours. Prevention focuses on repellent use and covering exposed skin. Schools should brief students before departure. No vaccine is currently required for entry.

**Road safety:** Thailand has significantly higher road traffic fatality rates than the UK or US. Students should be briefed on crossing roads safely and on the risks of riding motorcycles. flyed uses only pre-vetted transport providers with seatbelt policies.

**Water activities:** Snorkelling, swimming, kayaking, and sailing each require specific risk assessment. Ask operators for their in-water safety protocols, instructor qualifications, and emergency response plans. All water activities should be covered separately in the risk assessment.

**Heat exhaustion:** Thailand is hot and humid year-round. Acclimatisation is important for students not used to tropical conditions. Outdoor activities should be scheduled for cooler parts of the day, and hydration breaks must be planned and enforced.

**Political situation:** Thailand's political situation is generally stable for tourists. Monitor FCDO (UK) and State Department (US) travel advisories in the months before travel.
