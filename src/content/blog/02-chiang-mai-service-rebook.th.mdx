The Mae Klang River crested two metres higher than normal in October. The school group arriving from Kuala Lumpur had 48 hours to reorganize a full week's program. What happened next shows why logistics flexibility matters as much as planning.

## The problem: one site, no access

The group's planned base outside Chiang Mai sat in a valley that became inaccessible when the main road turned into a river. Their service project — a school-library renovation — was at the community centre across the river. Four days out, the itinerary looked broken.

## What the ground team did

Flyed's local coordinator in Chiang Mai contacted three backup partner organizations by 7am the next morning. By noon, the team had identified a viable alternative: a permaculture garden build at a hill-tribe cooperative 40 minutes away, with existing infrastructure and a community partner who had worked with student groups before.

The school leadership faced a choice: cancel the service component entirely, or pivot. They chose the pivot.

## What the students built instead

Over five days, the 22 students constructed a terraced herb and vegetable garden for the cooperative. The work was different from the original plan but no less meaningful — the cooperative feeds 34 families and had listed garden improvement as a goal for two years. Students learned swale-building, composting, and companion planting, finishing with a harvest they shared with the community.

## Why the rebook worked

The coordinator had pre-vetted four backup sites during the original planning phase. This is standard practice for northern Thailand programs during the wet season, but many operators skip it to save time. The difference between a trip that derails and one that adapts is often just a spreadsheet with four rows instead of one.

## What the school changed afterward

The school now requires all their overseas trip vendors to submit wet-season contingency plans for any destination with a rainy season. It became a standing item on their risk-assessment template.

A service-learning trip is never really about the original plan. It is about what the community needs when you arrive, and whether your program can meet that moment.
