Thailand's Andaman coast offers one of Southeast Asia's most accessible and spectacular maritime environments for school groups. Khao Lak, Phang Nga Bay, and the Similan Islands provide sailing conditions, coral ecosystems, and coastal geography that support genuine learning alongside genuine adventure. Here is how flyed structures sailing and marine-sports programs for school groups.

## Why the Andaman coast works for school sailing programs

The Andaman Sea has a defined sailing season from November to April, with conditions that are challenging enough for skill development but manageable for supervised student groups. Wind patterns during these months are consistent — typically 10 to 18 knots — and the water is calm between wind events.

Beyond sailing, the region offers kayaking through limestone karst caves, snorkelling on coral formations, and coastal ecology sessions that tie directly into GCSE, IGCSE, and IB science curricula. The Phang Nga Bay limestone geology is a natural case study for geology and earth science students.

## Program structure

flyed's Andaman sailing itinerary runs for 7 days. The program is designed for students aged 14 to 18 with no previous sailing experience required.

**Day 1–2: skills foundation.** Students receive classroom and on-water instruction in sailing basics, water safety, and marine conservation. Training takes place in sheltered waters near Khao Lak with qualified RYA instructors. By the end of day 2, students can tack, gybe, and sail a basic course.

**Day 3–5: island hopping.** The group sails between islands in Phang Nga Bay, stopping at designated anchorages each evening. Daily activities include snorkelling on coral reefs, kayaking through sea caves, and evening ecology sessions covering coral biology, fish identification, and the impacts of climate change on Indo-Pacific reef systems.

**Day 6: independent passage.** Students plan and execute a short independent passage with instructor supervision. This is the program's practical assessment — students must demonstrate navigation, crew communication, and decision-making under real conditions.

**Day 7: debrief and reflection.** The group returns to Khao Lak for a full debrief, marine science data presentation, and certificate ceremony.

## Marine science integration

The itinerary is designed to carry curriculum relevance beyond physical skills. Students collect reef health data using the CoralWatch monitoring protocol, record fish species during snorkel sessions, and analyse water quality samples. This data is shared with local marine park authorities, giving students a genuine contribution to ongoing conservation work.

For IB environmental science students, the program provides field work hours and primary data for their internal assessment. For GCSE geography students, the coastal management module connects directly to the issues affecting Phang Nga Bay's marine protected areas.

## Safety and instructor ratios

All sailing activities are led by RYA-qualified instructors with additional trained staff maintaining a 1-to-5 instructor-to-student ratio on the water. All boats carry VHF radios, GPS, and first-aid kits. Students wear personal flotation devices at all times on deck and during all water activities.

Instructors conduct a daily weather and sea-condition check. If conditions exceed safe thresholds, water activities are modified or relocated — this is a standing instruction that overrides any pre-planned itinerary.

## What teachers tell us after the trip

The most common feedback is that students who had never been on water before leave with both sailing competence and a tangible interest in marine conservation. The combination of skill acquisition and real scientific data collection creates engagement that a classroom lesson on coral bleaching simply cannot replicate.

The program runs from November through April. Booking 12 weeks in advance is required for marine park permits and instructor scheduling.
