Two days in Bangkok is not enough to see the city. It is enough to understand it — if the itinerary is structured around the right questions. Bangkok is one of the world's most instructive case studies for urban geography, economic development, and political geography. The challenge is not finding the content. The challenge is not letting the Khao San Road crowd out everything else.

## What Bangkok teaches

Bangkok's urbanisation is a primary case in the IB Geography syllabus — the Bangkok case study appears in the urban systems and urban environmental stress options. The city is also a standard example in development economics: rapid industrialisation, massive rural-urban migration, dual-sector models, and the informal economy all play out visibly in Bangkok's street-level landscape.

For Global Politics students, Bangkok's role as ASEAN headquarters and its 2014–2023 military government period make it a live case for authoritarianism and democratic transition. For Economics students, the city is a manufacturing and services hub with a land-value gradient that illustrates urban economic theory in real space.

## Day 1: Structure and inequality

Morning: Canal and market boat tour through Khlong Toei, Bangkok's largest low-income district and port area. Students observe the informal settlement patterns along the canal banks, the wet market operations, and the mix of residential, commercial, and industrial land use that characterises inner-city Bangkok. This is not a tourist canal tour — it is a guided urban field walk with structured observation tasks.

Afternoon: Visit Chatuchak Weekend Market as a case study in informal economic activity. Chatuchak generates an estimated 5 billion baht in weekly sales and employs tens of thousands of people in the informal sector. Students complete an economic survey exercise — categorising vendors by product type, estimating stall costs, and calculating implied revenue.

Evening: BTS Skytrain ride through the Sukhumvit corridor at rush hour. Students observe the urban land-value gradient from street level to elevated rail — the city visibly organises itself around the BTS and MRT lines in a way that urban economic theory predicts.

## Day 2: Governance and environment

Morning: Visit to the Bangkok Metropolitan Administration (BMA) flood management centre. Bangkok sits 1.5 metres above sea level and faces increasing tidal flooding as sea levels rise. The BMA's flood management infrastructure is a live engineering and governance case study. Students receive a briefing from BMA urban planning staff.

Afternoon: Visit to Thammasat University's Rangsit campus, which sits adjacent to the Bang Kadi canal restoration project — a live environmental remediation project that illustrates urban stream restoration as urban environmental management.

Evening: Debriefing session at the hotel. Students draft a structured field-note analysis using the IB Geography geographic skills framework — spatial awareness, data presentation, and evaluation.

## Logistics for a 48-hour school program

Accommodation matters. Stay in the Sukhumvit or Silom districts, within walking distance of BTS or MRT stations. Transit time is the enemy of a short itinerary — every hour spent in Bangkok traffic is an hour not spent in the field.

Meals are best taken at local restaurants near field sites, not at the hotel. The point is exposure to urban food culture, not comfort. The street food economy is itself a case study.

A 48-hour program requires ruthless prioritisation. Three sites done well beats six sites done as a circuit. The itinerary above works for IB Geography and Economics simultaneously. For a single-subject group, adjust depth accordingly.
