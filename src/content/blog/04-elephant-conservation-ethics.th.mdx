Elephant tourism is one of the most ethically complicated categories in travel education. Done well, it funds genuine conservation and gives students real exposure to animal welfare science. Done badly, it funds exploitation and teaches students the wrong lesson about human-animal relationships.

Most educators do not have the background to distinguish between the two. Here is the checklist that matters.

## The question most operators do not want to answer

Start with this: does the sanctuary breed, buy, or sell elephants? Any yes disqualifies the facility from consideration. Captive breeding programs for tourism use elephants as props. Legitimate sanctuaries exist to care for elephants that cannot return to the wild — they do not add to their population.

## Welfare indicators that educators should check

Elephants in poor conditions show specific signs. Look for: bobbing or weaving behaviour, listlessness during heat hours, chained limbs, and direct handling by visitors. These are welfare red flags regardless of what the sanctuary marketing says.

A legitimate operation will let you visit unannounced. Ask to see their USDA (or equivalent) animal welfare documentation. Ask how many elephants are currently in the program and what their health records look like. A transparent program welcomes these questions.

## The mahout question

Mahouts are elephant keepers. In legitimate sanctuaries, they are trained professionals who have moved away from riding programs. In exploitative operations, mahouts use bull hooks and the riding experience is the product.

Ask whether visitors handle bull hooks. If yes, walk away. Ask whether mahouts demonstrate natural elephant behaviour interpretation. If the answer is vague, ask for specifics.

## Community ownership and benefit

Who owns the land? Who employs the local community? A sanctuary that funnels profits to an overseas holding company does not provide the economic incentive to maintain high welfare standards. Local ownership with transparent revenue sharing is the model that sustains both elephant welfare and community investment.

## Budget as a signal

Elephant welfare is expensive. Elephants eat 200 kilograms of food per day. Medical care is specialist. A sanctuary offering deeply discounted student rates is subsidising that discount somehow — either through volume exploitation or through revenue from riding programs. Budget and ethics are directly connected here.

The educators who do this well spend more time on the front end of selection and less time managing student disappointment on arrival. The question is not whether the elephants look healthy. The question is whether the operation has a credible path to elephant welfare as its primary goal.
